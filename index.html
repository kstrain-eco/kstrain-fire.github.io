Current Research

My research interests lie at the intersection of invasion ecology, disturbance ecology, and conservation. I am interested in understanding how wildfire, elevational gradients, and climate change affect plant community assembly within an ecosystem.

Community effects of a non-native grass in East-Central Texas

We surveyed savannas and grasslands in fifteen privately owned properties across six counties east of Austin, Texas. Paspalum notatum, a range improvement grass introduced from South america was the most common non-native species, despite differences among sites in soils and woody cover. P. notatum was found to be associated with low vasucalar species richness. Our findings indicate that P. notatum should be classified as an invasive species in this region.

Plant and ecosystem responses to fire and invasion

As larger and more frequent fires continue to shape the landscape in the western United States and Great Masin, understanding the effect of fire on ecosystem processes and plant community assemply becomes increasingly important. We are currently monitoring post-fire recovery of vegetation in Lamoille Canyon near Elko, NV, to better understand the effect of burn presecnce and severity, time since fire, and herbaceous species diveristy on soil microbial activity and nutrient dynamics.
